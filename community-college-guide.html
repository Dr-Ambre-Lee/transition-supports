Transition Resource Guide

For Seniors
Planning to Attend Community College or Trade School

Introduction

This guide will help you navigate the transition from high school to community college or trade school. It includes information about local resources in Granada Hills and the San Fernando Valley, important steps to take before graduation, and contact information for agencies that can support your success.

Timeline: Senior Year Action Steps

Fall Semester (September - December)

  • Meet with your high school transition coordinator or special education case manager
  • Research community colleges and trade schools in your area
  • Schedule campus tours and meet with Disabled Student Programs & Services (DSPS)
  • Gather documentation of your disability (IEP, psychological evaluations, medical records)

Winter/Spring Semester (January - May)

  • Register with CCCApply for all community college applications
  • Submit college applications (deadlines vary, but many accept rolling applications)
  • Apply to Department of Rehabilitation (DOR) for adult services during spring semester
  • Complete FAFSA (Free Application for Federal Student Aid) with your family (Due March 2nd)
  • Apply for California College Promise Grant (CCPG) for fee waivers
  • Register with college DSPS office and request accommodations
  • Take college placement tests (if required)
  • Attend new student orientation

Summer Before College (June - August)

  • Finalize your fall class schedule with an academic counselor
  • Meet with DSPS to confirm accommodations are in place
  • Purchase textbooks and supplies
  • Arrange transportation to campus
  • Attend summer bridge programs if available

Key Support Agencies

Department of Rehabilitation (DOR) - Apply in Spring Semester

What they do: DOR provides job training, career counseling, assistive technology, and job placement services for people with disabilities. Students should apply during spring semester of senior year for adult services to begin after graduation.

Contact: San Fernando Valley Office, 9550 Topanga Canyon Blvd, Chatsworth, CA 91311 | (818) 717-7100

Regional Center of the San Fernando Valley

What they do: Regional Centers provide case management, assessments, and connections to services for individuals with developmental disabilities.

Contact: 7208 N Bellaire Ave, North Hollywood, CA 91605 | (818) 778-1900

WorkSource Centers (America's Job Center)

What they do: Free job training, career counseling, resume help, and computer access. Open to everyone, including students with mild disabilities.

Contact: Northeast Valley WorkSource Center, 8134 Van Nuys Blvd #200, Panorama City, CA 91402 | (818) 890-5416

Independent Living Center of Southern California

What they do: Provides skills training for independent living, self-advocacy, peer support, and community integration resources.

Contact: Van Nuys Office, 14407 Gilmore St, Van Nuys, CA 91401 | (818) 785-6934

Social Security Administration (SSA)

What they do: Manages SSI and SSDI benefits. Families should understand how work affects benefits and learn about the Ticket to Work program.

Contact: Van Nuys Social Security Office, 6351 Van Nuys Blvd, Van Nuys, CA 91401 | 1-800-772-1213

Understanding Disabled Student Programs & Services (DSPS)

DSPS is the college office that provides accommodations and support services for students with disabilities. Every California community college has a DSPS office.

What Services Does DSPS Provide?

  • Extended time on tests and assignments
  • Assistive technology (screen readers, speech-to-text software, calculators)
  • Note-taking services
  • Preferential seating
  • Reduced course load without losing full-time status
  • Academic counseling and educational planning
  • Priority registration
  • Learning disability assessments (if needed)

How to Register with DSPS

  • Contact the DSPS office at your chosen college
  • Schedule an intake appointment
  • Bring documentation of your disability (IEP, psychological evaluation, doctor's note)
  • Meet with a DSPS counselor to create your accommodation plan
  • Provide accommodation letters to your professors each semester

Financial Aid & Fee Waivers

Free Application for Federal Student Aid (FAFSA)

Complete the FAFSA with your family to determine eligibility for federal grants, loans, and work-study. California community college students may qualify for the Pell Grant, which does not need to be repaid. FAFSA opens October 1st each year for the following academic year.

Website: www.studentaid.gov

California College Promise Grant (CCPG)

Previously called the BOG Fee Waiver, this grant covers enrollment fees at California community colleges for eligible students. Apply through your college's financial aid office.

Other Scholarships

  • Check with your high school counselor about local scholarships
  • Ask your college financial aid office about disability-specific scholarships
  • Search online databases like Fastweb.com and Scholarships.com

Important Documents to Gather

Having these documents organized will make the transition process much smoother:

  • High school transcripts
  • Current Individualized Education Program (IEP)
  • Psychological or educational evaluations (within last 3 years)
  • Medical documentation of disability (if applicable)
  • Social Security card and birth certificate
  • California ID or driver's license
  • Immunization records

Key Differences Between High School & College

High School College
School manages your supports through your IEP You must self-advocate and request accommodations
Teachers check in on your progress Professors expect you to manage your own time and reach out for help
Classes meet every day Classes may meet 2-3 times per week with more independent study
Attendance is often required by law Attendance policies vary by professor; absences affect your grade
Free education with provided materials You pay for tuition, books, and materials (financial aid available)

Tips for Success in College

  • Start small: Take 2-3 classes your first semester to adjust to the workload
  • Use your accommodations: Give accommodation letters to professors on the first day
  • Build relationships: Introduce yourself to professors during office hours
  • Use campus resources: Tutoring centers, writing centers, and study groups are free
  • Stay organized: Use a planner, phone calendar, or app to track assignments and deadlines
  • Ask for help early: Don't wait until you're failing to reach out for support
  • Take care of yourself: Get enough sleep, eat well, and make time for activities you enjoy

Transition Resource Guide

For Seniors
Planning to Attend a Four-Year College or University

Introduction

Congratulations on planning to attend a four-year college! This guide will help you navigate the transition from high school to university. It includes information about college disability services, the application process, important steps to take before graduation, and resources to support your success.

Timeline: Senior Year Action Steps

Fall Semester (September - December)

  • Meet with your high school transition coordinator or special education case manager
  • Meet with your college and career counselor
  • Research colleges and visit campuses (schedule tours of disability services offices)
  • Take SAT/ACT if required by your target colleges (request accommodations through College Board/ACT)
  • Start working on college applications and personal statements
  • Request letters of recommendation from teachers and counselors
  • Gather documentation of your disability (IEP, psychological evaluations, medical records)
  • Submit early action/early decision applications (if applicable, usually November deadlines)

Spring Semester (January - May)

  • Submit regular decision college applications (usually January-February deadlines)
  • Complete FAFSA (Free Application for Federal Student Aid) with your family
  • Submit CSS Profile if required by your colleges
  • Apply for scholarships (local, national, and disability-specific)
  • Apply to Department of Rehabilitation (DOR) for adult services during spring semester
  • Receive college acceptance letters and compare financial aid packages (March-April)
  • Make your final college decision and submit enrollment deposit (usually by May 1st)
  • Contact your college's disability services office to start registration process
  • Submit disability documentation to your college's disability services office
  • Meet with disability services counselor to establish accommodations
  • Attend admitted student events and orientation programs

Summer Before College (June - August)

  • Complete housing application and request any needed accommodations (accessible room, emotional support animal, etc.)
  • Register for first semester classes (may include early registration as an accommodation)
  • Attend new student orientation and meet with academic advisor
  • Purchase textbooks and supplies
  • If applicable, arrange for ongoing medical care near campus (therapist, psychiatrist, doctors)

The College Application Process

Should You Disclose Your Disability?

Important: You are NOT required to disclose your disability in your college application. Admissions decisions are made separately from disability services.

Consider disclosing if:

  • Your disability significantly impacted your academic record (lower grades, gaps in coursework)
  • Overcoming challenges related to your disability demonstrates resilience and growth
  • You want to explain your unique perspective or experiences

Application Components

  • Common Application or college-specific application
  • Personal statement/essays - Share your story, goals, and what makes you unique
  • High school transcripts
  • Standardized test scores (SAT/ACT) - Many schools are now test-optional
  • Letters of recommendation - From teachers, counselors, or mentors
  • Application fee or fee waiver - Fee waivers available for students with financial need

Understanding College Disability Services

What Services Are Available?

College disability services vary by institution but typically include:

  • Extended time on exams and assignments
  • Reduced distraction testing environment
  • Note-taking assistance or recording lectures
  • Assistive technology (screen readers, speech-to-text software, FM systems)
  • Priority registration for classes
  • Reduced course load while maintaining full-time status
  • Academic advising and coaching
  • Housing accommodations (single room, accessible room, air conditioning, emotional support animal)
  • Meal plan accommodations (if you have dietary restrictions related to disability)

How to Register for Accommodations

  • Contact your college's disability services office as soon as you're admitted
  • Complete their intake process and submit required documentation
  • Documentation typically needs to be from a qualified professional and include diagnosis, functional limitations, and recommended accommodations
  • Meet with a disability services counselor to create your accommodation plan
  • Request accommodation letters each semester and provide them to your professors

What they do: DOR can help pay for college-related expenses such as tuition, books, assistive technology, and transportation. They also provide career counseling and job placement services. Apply during your spring semester of senior year for services to begin when you start college.

Financial Aid & Scholarships

Free Application for Federal Student Aid (FAFSA)

Complete the FAFSA with your family to determine eligibility for federal grants (Pell Grant, FSEOG), loans, and work-study programs. FAFSA typically opens October 1st each year for the following academic year.

Website: www.studentaid.gov

CSS Profile

Many private colleges and some public universities require the CSS Profile in addition to FAFSA for institutional aid. Check each college's financial aid website for requirements.

Website: www.cssprofile.collegeboard.org

California State University Grant Programs

  • Cal Grant: State grant for California residents attending California colleges
  • Middle Class Scholarship: For families earning up to $217,000 attending UC or CSU

Disability-Specific Scholarships

  • Google Lime Scholarship (for students with disabilities pursuing computer science)
  • Anne Ford and Allegra Ford Thomas Scholarships (for students with learning disabilities)
  • National Federation of the Blind Scholarships
  • Autism Society of America Scholarships
  • Organization for Autism Research Scholarship

Department of Rehabilitation (DOR) Financial Support

If approved for DOR services, they may help pay for tuition, books, transportation, assistive technology, and other education-related expenses. This is particularly helpful if you don't qualify for enough financial aid.

Important Documents to Gather

Having these documents organized will make the transition process much smoother:

  • High school transcripts (official and unofficial copies)
  • SAT/ACT score reports (if applicable)
  • Current Individualized Education Program (IEP)
  • Psychological or educational evaluations (within last 3 years for college disability services)
  • Medical documentation of disability from qualified professional
  • List of current medications and prescriptions
  • Social Security card and birth certificate
  • California ID or driver's license
  • Immunization records (required by most colleges)
  • Health insurance information

Key Differences Between High School & College

High School College
School provides services through your IEP You must request accommodations and provide documentation
Teachers monitor your progress and reach out Professors expect you to self-advocate and ask for help
Parents are involved in your education You are responsible for your education (FERPA limits parent access)
Structured daily schedule (same classes every day) Flexible schedule with classes 2-3 times per week; you manage your time
Most testing is frequent and covers small amounts of material Tests are less frequent and cover more material; finals may be 30-40% of grade
Free education with provided materials You pay tuition, fees, housing, books, and living expenses

Living on Campus vs. Commuting

Benefits of Living on Campus

  • Closer to classes, library, and campus resources
  • More opportunities to make friends and participate in campus activities
  • Develops independence and life skills
  • Access to resident advisors and dormitory support

Benefits of Commuting from Home

  • More affordable (saves on room and board costs)
  • Familiar environment and family support
  • Easier to maintain ongoing medical care and therapy
  • Less overwhelming transition for some students

Consider: Many students with disabilities start by commuting and move to campus housing after a year once they're comfortable with college.

Tips for Success in College

  • Register with disability services immediately: Don't wait until you're struggling. Get accommodations in place from day one.
  • Communicate with professors: Visit during office hours, introduce yourself, and provide your accommodation letter early in the semester.
  • Use campus resources: Tutoring centers, writing centers, academic coaches, counseling services - they're all free and there to help you succeed.
  • Build a support network: Connect with other students with disabilities, join clubs, find a mentor.
  • Manage your time: Use planners, apps, or calendars. For every hour in class, plan 2-3 hours of study time.
  • Start with a manageable course load: Taking 12-13 units is full-time. Don't overload yourself your first semester.
  • Practice self-care: Sleep, nutrition, exercise, and mental health are as important as academics.
  • Know when to ask for help: If you're falling behind, reach out immediately - don't wait until finals week.
  • Be your own advocate: No one will manage your education for you. Speak up for what you need.
